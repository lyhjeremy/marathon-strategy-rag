# The Heat Tax: How Temperature Slows Your Marathon
> Source: Jeremy Lee's Boston Marathon weather analysis

Heat is one of the most powerful and predictable forces acting on marathon times — and it is easy to underestimate on race morning.

**What 14 years of Boston data reveal (weather-paired):**
- Per-year median finish time versus race-day midday temperature correlates at **r=0.86** — a very strong relationship.
- The slope is approximately **1 minute slower per °F** of midday temperature.
- Going from **50°F to 80°F** adds roughly **31 minutes** to the median finish time.

**Why heat costs so much:**
- Your body diverts blood flow to the skin for cooling, leaving less for working muscles.
- Core temperature rises, heart rate drifts upward at the same pace, and dehydration compounds the effect.
- The penalty grows nonlinearly as temperatures climb into the 70s and 80s °F.

**What this means for you:**
- **Adjust your goal pace upward on hot days.** A cool-weather PR goal is simply the wrong target when it is 75–80°F.
- Check the forecast in the final days and set a realistic "hot-day" pace band.
- Start more conservatively, prioritize hydration and cooling (ice, sponges, shade), and accept a slower time rather than blowing up.

**Actionable takeaway:** Don't chase a cool-weather goal in the heat. Use roughly a **1 minute per °F** rule of thumb above comfortable conditions to reset expectations, then race the day you're given.
