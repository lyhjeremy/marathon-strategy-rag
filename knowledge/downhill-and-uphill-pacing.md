# Downhill and Uphill Pacing
> Source: Established marathon training principles

On hilly and rolling courses, chasing a constant *pace* wastes energy. The right approach is to run by **constant effort**, letting pace vary with the terrain.

**Uphills:**
- **Hold effort steady, let pace slow.** Fighting to maintain flat-ground pace uphill spikes heart rate and burns fuel disproportionately.
- Shorten your stride, keep cadence up, and stay relaxed. You'll lose a little time going up and can gently recover it over the top.

**Downhills:**
- Ease off the effort and let gravity add some speed — but **don't hammer descents.** Downhill running is **eccentric loading** that causes muscle damage, especially to the **quads**.
- Accumulated quad damage from early downhills shows up as trashed legs late in the race — a defining challenge of **Boston**, whose net-downhill first half can wreck quads before the Newton hills at ~30 km.
- Control your stride, avoid overstriding and braking, and keep turnover quick.

**Rolling courses:**
- Think in terms of **average effort**, not mile-by-mile pace. Expect splits to fluctuate and don't panic on the slow uphill miles.
- **Train specifically for downhills** if your race has them: downhill reps build eccentric resistance so your quads survive the back half.

**Actionable takeaway:** Run hills by effort, not pace. Give back a little time uphill, resist the urge to bomb downhills, and — for courses like Boston — train your quads on descents so late-race hills don't find you with dead legs.
