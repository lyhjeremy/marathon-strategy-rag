# Hitting the Wall: Where the Marathon Is Really Decided
> Source: Jeremy Lee's Boston Marathon pacing-decay analysis

"The wall" is not a myth or a metaphor — it is a measurable inflection point in pace, and the data pinpoint where it happens.

**What the Boston data show (31,655 finishers):**
- Pace stays roughly flat through about **20 km**.
- From **~30 km** onward, pace climbs steeply — a clear, population-wide inflection.
- Median late-race (35–40 km) slowdown is **15%**.
- **27%** of finishers slowed by more than **25%** in the closing kilometers.

**The effect scales with finish time:**
- Sub-3:00 runners stay nearly flat to the finish.
- Runners at 4:30+ commonly slow **30% or more** late in the race.

**Why it happens:** Glycogen stores deplete, fatigue accumulates, and any pace overspending from the early miles comes due all at once in the 30–42 km segment.

**What this means for you:**
- The race is won or lost in the final **30–42 km**, not the first 30.
- **Bank fitness and fuel, not time.** Time banked early by running fast is almost always lost with interest after 30 km.
- Structure your training and fueling specifically for the back third: long runs that rehearse fatigue, and a fueling plan that keeps glycogen topped up before depletion sets in.

**Actionable takeaway:** Treat 30 km as the true start of your race. Run the first 30 km conservatively enough that you still have pace in reserve when everyone around you is decelerating.
