# Even Pacing Strategy
> Source: Established marathon training principles

For the vast majority of marathoners, **even pacing** — holding a steady effort and pace throughout — produces the best result. A slightly negative split (finishing marginally faster) is the ideal ceiling, but even splitting is the practical target.

**Why even pacing wins:**
- Running too fast early burns glycogen and generates fatigue disproportionately, which you repay with a steep slowdown in the final 10–12 km.
- **Banking time early is a trap.** Time gained by going out fast is almost always lost — with interest — late in the race.
- A steady pace keeps energy expenditure efficient and delays the wall.

**How to set your goal pace:**
- Base it on **recent race results and long-run/tempo paces**, not hope. A recent half marathon is a strong predictor.
- Adjust for **conditions** (heat, wind) and **course** (hills, elevation profile) — don't apply a flat-course goal to a hard course or a hot day.
- Pick a pace you're confident you can hold for the *whole* distance, then run the first few miles a touch slower.

**Executing it:**
- Deliberately hold back in the opening 5–10 km when adrenaline makes goal pace feel easy.
- Check splits, but race by effort as fatigue builds.
- Aim to reach halfway feeling controlled, with pace still in reserve.

**Actionable takeaway:** Set a realistic, condition-adjusted goal pace and run it evenly. Discipline in the first third — resisting the urge to bank time — is the strongest predictor of a strong finish.
