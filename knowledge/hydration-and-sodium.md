# Hydration and Sodium
> Source: Established marathon training principles

Staying appropriately hydrated helps maintain pace and thermoregulation — but both under- and over-drinking carry real risks.

**Drink to thirst:**
- For most runners, **drinking to thirst** is a safe and effective guide. Thirst is a reliable signal; you do not need to force fluids on a rigid schedule.
- Fluid needs rise sharply in heat and for larger, faster, or heavier-sweating runners.

**Sodium and electrolytes:**
- Sweat contains sodium. On longer, hotter efforts, replacing some **sodium/electrolytes** (via sports drinks or salt tablets) helps maintain fluid balance and can reduce cramping for heavy or salty sweaters.
- Plain water alone over many hours, especially with overdrinking, can dilute blood sodium.

**The hyponatremia risk:**
- **Hyponatremia** — dangerously low blood sodium — comes from **overdrinking**, not underdrinking. It can be serious or fatal.
- Warning signs include nausea, headache, confusion, and bloating/weight *gain* during the race.
- Avoid it by not drinking far beyond thirst and by including sodium on long, hot efforts.

**Heat considerations:**
- In heat, prioritize both fluids and electrolytes, and add external cooling (ice, sponges) — you cannot fully "drink away" heat stress.

**Actionable takeaway:** Drink to thirst, include sodium on long or hot races, and never force large volumes of plain water. If you're gaining weight or feel bloated and foggy, drink less, not more.
