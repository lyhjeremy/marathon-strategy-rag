# The Long Run
> Source: Established marathon training principles

The long run is the cornerstone of marathon training. It builds the aerobic engine and the fatigue resistance that carry you through the final, hardest miles.

**What it develops:**
- **Aerobic base:** more capillaries, mitochondria, and improved fat oxidation, so you spare glycogen at a given pace.
- **Fatigue resistance:** teaching your legs and mind to keep working when tired.
- **Fueling practice:** a real-world lab for rehearsing gels, drinks, and gut tolerance.
- **Musculoskeletal durability:** conditioning tendons, joints, and muscles to time on feet.

**Typical progression:**
- Build long-run distance **gradually**, often adding modestly week to week with periodic **step-back (recovery) weeks** to absorb the load.
- Many plans peak the long run somewhere around **32 km (20 miles)** or a time cap (e.g. 2.5–3 hours) a few weeks before race day.
- Most long runs are run at an **easy, conversational pace**; some plans add segments at goal marathon pace to rehearse race effort.

**Guidelines:**
- Prioritize consistency over any single heroic run — the cumulative effect matters most.
- Don't let the long run become so long or fast that it compromises the rest of the week's training or recovery.
- Practice race-day logistics: fueling timing, hydration, shoes, and pacing.

**Actionable takeaway:** Build your long run steadily with step-back weeks, keep most of it easy, use it to rehearse fueling and race-pace segments, and let consistency — not one huge effort — build your marathon endurance.
