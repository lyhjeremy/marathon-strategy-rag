# In-Race Carbohydrate Fueling
> Source: Established marathon training principles

Your muscles and brain run on carbohydrate, but your body stores only enough glycogen for roughly 90–120 minutes of hard running. When those stores run low, pace collapses — this is a major cause of "the wall." In-race fueling delays that depletion.

**How much to take:**
- A common target is **~30–60 g of carbohydrate per hour** for most marathoners.
- Well-trained guts using a **mixed glucose:fructose** blend (roughly 2:1) can absorb up to **~90 g/hr**, because glucose and fructose use different intestinal transporters.
- Faster runners racing longer generally aim toward the higher end.

**Timing:**
- **Start early** — within the first 30–45 minutes — not when you already feel empty. Once you're depleted, it's too late to catch up.
- Fuel at **regular intervals** (e.g. every 20–30 minutes) rather than in one large dose.

**Practice it:**
- The gut is trainable. **Rehearse your exact fueling plan in long runs** at race pace to build tolerance and avoid GI distress.
- Test the specific gels, chews, or drinks you'll use on race day, including the on-course sports drink if you'll rely on it.

**Actionable takeaway:** Choose a per-hour carb target, front-load the start of your fueling, use a glucose-fructose mix if going high, and never introduce a new product on race day. Fueling is a skill you train, not a race-day improvisation.
